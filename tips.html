Taiwan is different, but easy. Try the below things for the most friction-free and fun trip.

Public Transit and Travel

Public Transit is Almost Always the Best Choice

You may be in the habit of renting a car at the same time you book your plane tickets and hotel. In Taiwan, that's not necessary, nor is it a very good idea - you'll be wasting money and creating unnecessary stress for yourself. For getting from the airport to the city, as well as inner-city travel, public transit is almost always the fastest solution, especially when you consider how long it takes to find parking.

Take the Express Train from the Airport to Taipei Main Station

The express train is the fastest, and cheapest, way to get from the airport to Taipei Main. It's designed for travelers and has plenty of room for luggage. Follow signs for the "MRT" in the airport to get there. Keep in mind, there's a non-express, and express train, that leave from the Airport. The Express train leaves about every 20 minutes and is worth the wait, since it makes far fewer stops and gets to Taipei Main much more quickly, always beating the non-express train that leaves before it. Pay close attention to digital signs hanging from the ceiling!

Pay for the MRT Rides with your credit card using tap to pay

The airport train, and the MRT, accepts credit cards tap-to-pay. If you want to use the Ubike or TRA (not necessarily likely), you'll need to get a Yoyo-card/Easycard. Head to any 7-11 or Family Mart and look for small racks of cards, usually near the check-out area. Don't Eat or Drink on Most Public Transit

Most trains you'll likely be on don't allow eating or drinking. This rule is strictly enforced, please don't try to get away with a little sip of your drink or anything like that, it will make people uncomfortable and may result in confrontation, or even an anonymous tip that causes station staff to accost you and give you a fine.

Keep about 300NTD on your EasyCard

Each train ride is under 30NTD, so 300 should be enough for plenty of rides. When it gets under 100, top it up at one of the kiosks in any MRT station, cash only. I put 1000NTD on my card, which usually lasts me several months.

Taiwan has Uber

Worse case scenario, you can always use Uber, which usually has a fast pickup time. However, you'll probably get stuck in traffic for a bit.

Yellow Cabs are Cheap and Safe

Yellow cabs in Taiwan are also an option, usually a bit cheaper than Uber, but safe and strictly regulated. Taiwan doesn't have issues with fake taxis or drivers not using the meter. However, you may struggle to communicate your destination to the driver, as they almost never speak any English. Google Maps usually has an option to speak an address in Mandarin, which usually works fine - Taxi Drivers have every street in the city memorized, and Taipei uses a relatively logical system of escalating Lanes, Alleys, and Sections that lets taxi drivers easily bring you to your exact location.

Money & Payments

Taiwan is a Cash-Heavy Country

It's almost certain that you'll go to businesses that don't accept credit cards - for example, no night market vendor does. Be ready to pay with cash. You'll probably want at least 1500NTD (~30USD) per person per day, although you can easily spend more if you're snacking a lot or paying for tickets for museums.

The ATM May be the Cheapest Way to Get Cash

Depending on your bank, using your debit card to withdraw cash at a Taiwan ATM may be the cheapest way to get cash. You could of course bring cash to exchange at an airport exchange, however you probably won't save much money this way, and may end up paying just as much as if you just eat a fee (3-10$ USD) for withdrawing from an ATM. Withdraw a large amount in one go, since the fee is usually per-transaction, not percentage-of-amount.

DO NOT TIP

There is absolutely zero tipping culture in Taiwan. You will never be in a situation in Taiwan where a tip is expected, and in fact any situation where you try to tip, you'll instead end up creating a very, very uncomfortable situation. Please, do NOT try to tip here. I promise you that no matter how good your intentions, you'll just end up in a super awkward situation where your money is refused, which you may misinterpret as simple politeness, which may make the situation even more uncomfortable as you continue to try to offer the money. Furthermore, the very offering of a tip may be deeply insulting to the person you're offering it to - you're completely cheapening whatever relationship you had with them by applying a cash value to it. I really can't stress how important it is to simply never, ever tip. Not in restaurants, not to taxi drivers, not to tour guides, not to hotel staff, never, ever try to do it. If you see a "tip" field on a receipt, it's because the restaurant bought the device or software from the USA, and they probably aren't even aware the field is there. Ignore it, and DO NOT TIP.

Food & Dining

Vegan and Vegetarian is Slightly Challenging

Historically, in Taiwan, "vegetarian" means you're a monk, which means you also don't eat garlic or onion, but are flexible with using meat-based broths or oils, in other words, not true vegetarianism. That means a dish listed as "vegetarian" may not actually be. However, the country has heavily modernized, so the english word "vegan" appearing on a menu means it's probably truly vegan.

That said, you can always in an emergency go to a 7-11 or Family Mart, which has well labeled snacks that let you ensure you're eating Vegan or Vegetarian. There are also recently a lot of vegetarian and even a few vegan restaurants sprouting up.

The Tap Water is "Drinkable" but You Probably Shouldn't

The water from the city main is purified and drinkable, however, the piping infrastructure in Taiwan is often lacking, so the water may be slightly off by the time it reaches your hotel room or wherever. Basically all hotels will have multiple purified water machines for you to use, so it's recommended to carry a water bottle to avoid plastic waste. Worse case, you can get large water bottles from a convenience store. You should have no concerns at all from showering or brushing your teeth with tap water. Public water fountains, such as in the MRT, are tested regularly and safe.

Weather & Packing

Temperature will be around 22-27C

Taiwan is a very hot country, and so fall temperatures will be much warmer than most places in the USA, and for Europeans, possibly even summer-like. That said, it's probably a good idea to bring a light jacket, since places such as the mountains or ocean-side can become quite cool in the fall.

Taiwan is Very Humid

Taiwanese homes and businesses often have problems with mold due to the high humidity. Be prepared to be feeling very humid the entire time you're here.

Communication

You'll be Fine Speaking English

While the majority of people in Taiwan don't speak much English, most of the people you'll interact with are prepared to deal with English speakers - they'll probably have English menus at restaurants, hotels will almost assuredly have English-speaking staff, and the MRT and other public transit is all multi-lingual (Mandarin, Taiwanese, English, and sometimes Japanese). There's no reason to stress about the need to learn any Mandarin.

Google Translate is Very Helpful

Download the google translate app on your phone and set "Chinese - Traditional" as downloaded for offline use. While in Taiwan, you can use the camera feature of Google Translate to translate signs or menus that aren't available in English.

Taiwanese People Speak Mandarin and write in Traditional Chinese

There are many languages spoken in Taiwan, but the most common language is Mandarin Chinese, written in Traditional Chinese. You may see this written as zh-Hant or zh-TW.

A List of Most Useful Words to Learn

The most useful terms to learn are as follows, in order of most important to least.

  • 謝謝 (Xièxiè) - Thank you
  • 你好 (Nǐ hǎo) - Hello
  • 對不起 (Duìbùqǐ) - Sorry
  • 多少錢 (Duōshǎo qián) - How much does it cost?
  • 廁所在哪裡 (Cèsuǒ zài nǎlǐ) - Where is the bathroom?
  • 請問 (Qǐngwèn) - Excuse me / May I ask
  • Numbers up to 9,999

Safety and General Environment

Taiwan is One of the Safest Countries in the World

Taiwan has some of the lowest rates of violent and property crime in the world. Mugging and pickpocketing is completely unheard of, you have absolutely no reason to fear for this. You don't need to be careful for your phone getting snatched on public transit or near a street, and in fact if you forget your wallet or purse somewhere, it will almost assuredly either not be touched by anyone for hours and hours, or be with the police or public transit staff.

You're perfectly safe walking around alone at night, in both highly populated as well as suburban areas.

Emergency Numbers

The police can be reached at 110, and the fire department / ambulance can be reached at 119. However, calling either number in an emergency is fine, the operator will get you to the right department, and they have English translators available.

Taiwan is Safe for LGBT People

Taiwan was the first country in Asia to legalize gay marriage. There's no issues with violence against LGBT people in Taiwan, and Taiwan has had gay and trans government officials in the past.

Taiwan has a Relatively High Traffic Fatality Rate

While you don't need to be at all worried about crime, you do need to be very alert when around traffic in Taiwan. Taiwan has rule-of-law on the road (unlike countries such as Vietnam or India, which are free-for-alls), however Taiwan also has very little pedestrian protections, and certain specific laws are ignored en masse every day, such as speed limits or rules for scooters passing cars and eachother. Furthermore, until very recently, cars were basically allowed to push pedestrians in crosswalks out of their way if they wanted. Things aren't that bad anymore, but there are still frequent pedestrian/vehicle accidents. Furthermore, pedestrian infrastructure is relatively undeveloped compared to countries like Japan - though it will probably be a lot better than most American cities other than San Francisco or New York.

A lot of sidewalks are just green painted areas on the road. If on one of these, stay very aware of traffic, and don't walk while looking at your phone. Try to walk against traffic so you can see the cars coming - cars love to drive on these, or drive close enough to them that their mirror will hit you. Also, scooters frequently use this as a passing lane.

When crossing roads, keep a SHARP eye out for vehicles turning or speeding to get through before the red light - many Taipei intersections are so big that cars that entered an intersection on a green light won't cross the pedestrian crosswalk on the other side of the intersection until the light has been red for several seconds.

There are certain vehicles that ignore all road rules. The first is the "Trash Grandma," an elderly person driving a tricycle scooter or similar pulling a very large container full of garbage. These pose no threat to you since they drive quite slow, however cars and scooters will often be driving very quickly and angrily to get around these vehicles. The second is the "blue truck," which are almost always driven by someone high on Betel Nut or simply drunk, and always so fast that they have no reasonable stopping distance. It's best to pay close attention to blue trucks and be ready to get out of their way.

Marijuana and other Related Substances are Strictly Illegal in All Forms

The only drugs that are legal in Taiwan are alcohol, betel nut, and caffeine. All other ones are treated as equally potent and illegal by authorities, similarly to how cocaine would be treated in the USA. Don't try to bring edibles or a vape into Taiwan, the penalty for being caught at the border is huge since you'll be accused of international drug trafficking. It's simply not worth the risk. Buying here probably isn't worth it, the quality is much lower than what you can get in the USA.

Police Stations Always Have Bathrooms and Water

If you're struggling to find a bathroom or water, police stations always have both (in the form of a water machine, not a bottle for you to take with you), and are required by law to let anyone use them. Police stations are open 24/7. Simply walk in and speak to the front desk officer, they'll probably understand "bathroom."

There's Some Public Restrooms in Taiwan

Here and there are public restrooms in Taiwan, even in strange places like in the middle of a suburban neighborhood. They're always free. Also, most MRT stations have bathrooms. If the bathroom is behind the ticket gate, you can ask the station staff to use the bathroom, and they'll let you in for free. Generally speaking, it's a good idea to always take advantage of a bathroom if you see one.

There Are No Guns in Taiwan

Ownership of a gun for civilians is essentially illegal. You should only see guns on police and enlisted military in uniform, and often not even then. If you see someone with a gun (a black swan event), it's probably a very bad situation, and you should call the police.

Avoid Swearing or Insulting People

Technically, defamation in Taiwan is a criminal matter, not civil. Swearing at someone can be considered defamation. It's unlikely, but to avoid legal trouble, try to avoid swearing at people or insulting them. It's a well-known trope that restaurants will even report people to the police for a 1 star review. As someone who's been through the legal process for this, for the most part, there aren't really consequences anymore since the police and prosecutors are tired of dealing with petty grievances like this, but it's best to just avoid the headache.

Culture and Behavior

Taiwan is a "First-World" Liberal Democracy

Most aspects of Taiwan infrastructure, bureaucracy, policing, and general environment will be familiar to people from western liberal democracies. The quality of life is very high, often rivaling the USA, and medical outcomes and average lifespan surpass that of the USA. It's generally clean here, and people have high hygiene standards. There are almost no homeless people, nor are there slums. Taiwan has what most Europeans and Americans would interpret as "freedom of speech."

Taiwan is a Very Polite Culture

Taiwanese people default to being polite, especially to strangers. Don't be afraid to ask for help from random people, they're almost always very friendly and happy to help, even if they're busy. While here, please match that energy, and don't be rude to people on the street, or to staff in businesses. Even if you're frustrated, angry behavior won't be tolerated as much as it is in countries like the USA - you're more likely to frighten everyone in the area and drastically reduce your chance of resolving whatever situation you're having.

How You Handle Money is Important

In Taiwan, any kind of paper being passed around, especially money, has somewhat subtle rules in terms of behavior. You may accidentally be quite rude without realizing it when you pay someone - though it's not that big of a deal in the end if you're visibly non-Taiwanese, as Taiwanese people are quite forgiving. When handing money to people, it's good to do so with both hands. It doesn't need to be overly formal, or accompanied by a bow, and it's not too big of a deal if you forget this, but as a general rule it's just more polite. At minimum, you shouldn't aggressively shove money to someone with one hand, or yank it from people with one hand. And of course, throwing money at people is, like in most places, incredibly rude.

Generally Speaking, Taiwanese People are a Bit More Quiet

Try to avoid shouting, raising your voice in anger or excitement, or talking very loudly in public, unless you're somewhere where this is expected like a concert, sporting event, or sports bar during a game (definitely not any other kind of bar though). Taiwan isn't quite like Japan in this sense, but loud voices will draw a lot of attention, and probably not in the way you want. In fact, it's almost guaranteed to have the cops called on you for a noise complaint if you sit outside a 7-11 drinking and chatting with your friends.

Drive and Walk on the Right

Taiwan walking and driving rules are identical to the USA in terms of direction.

Men: There May be an Old Woman in the Bathroom

Taiwanese cleaning staff are often "Aunties," and they don't seem to care if you're using the bathroom, they'll come clean anyway. Just do your best to ignore them. There's not much point in waiting for them to leave, they seem to always be around doing something or another.

Taiwan is a Gender Egalitarian Country

Taiwan for the most part doesn't draw a distinction between men and women in day to day life. If you make comments about women or men being suited for certain kinds of labor, or anything that may be construed as sexist, it will be jarring and create a very uncomfortable situation. On that note, it's socially acceptable for women to discuss periods more openly than in the USA. Usually not to strangers, but Taiwanese friends may mention having a bad day because of their period, for example.

Taiwanese Fashion is a Bit More Conservative

Especially for women, fashion usually avoids exposing a lot of skin. However, there's not really any rule against for example having a very low cut shirt or showing a lot of midriff - some Taiwanese people will do this. Still, it will draw attention. Since Taiwan is generally safe, it probably won't draw catcalls, aggressive staring, or aggressive approach, but, people might keep glancing at you or be surprised if they see you when they step on the train.

Someone Might Call You Fat as a Compliment

Taiwanese people are much more forward about commenting on someone's body weight. Usually, it's meant in a friendly or familiar manner, or perhaps even a compliment (drawing comparisons to a fat friendly "Buddha" personality, for example). It's quite annoying, but please don't take it personally, if someone is calling you fat, they almost certainly are trying to be friendly or comfortable with you.

Everyone Here is Very Tired of Tourists Asking Them About the PRC ("China")

For whatever reason everyone always wants to ask about this - it seems western media is scaremongering about PRC invasion constantly. To answer your question: No, nobody here is worried about the PRC invading. No, we're not worried about PLA jets or boats crossing whatever line they're supposed to have crossed this time. No, invasion is not imminent, yes, we know Xi said 2027, he has said similar in the past. No, you will not be suddenly blown up by a PLA bomb in a surprise invasion. Invasion of Taiwan would require troop movement of millions of bodies - impossible to hide from satellite and Taiwanese spies. No, the PRC would never nuke Taiwan, remember that it considers us a province and the people here members of their newly invented state-race "Chinese." The PRC is attempting economic and cultural takeover of Taiwan, or political malfeasance by leveraging the KMT, there's almost no chance of invasion until those efforts completely fail. Also, don't call the PRC "Mainland China," call it either "China" or "the PRC," or people will be slightly annoyed with you. If you want people to love you a lot, call it "West Taiwan."
